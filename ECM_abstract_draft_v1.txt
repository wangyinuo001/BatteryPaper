Abstract

Accurate battery time-to-empty (TTE) prediction remains challenging for smartphones due to the coupled effects of electrochemical nonlinearity, dynamic component-level loads, temperature variation, and aging-induced degradation. This study proposes a unified continuous-time framework that jointly models (i) terminal-voltage dynamics using an electrochemically interpretable formulation, (ii) component-wise power decomposition of screen, SoC, radio, GPS, and baseline loads, and (iii) temperature- and cycle-dependent parameter evolution for capacity fade and resistance growth. The model parameters are identified from publicly available XJTU and NASA lithium-ion battery datasets and then integrated into a scenario-level runtime prediction pipeline.

Compared with a parsimonious Nernst-based baseline, the proposed framework provides substantially improved voltage-tracking accuracy (RMSE reduction up to 75.5%) and more stable error behavior across the full state-of-charge range. Under representative usage patterns, the predicted TTE is 22.69 h (standby), 9.07 h (navigation), 6.42 h (video streaming), 9.69 h (reading), and 3.84 h (online gaming), where gaming shows the fastest depletion due to concurrent high SoC, screen, and radio demand. Sensitivity and assumption analyses indicate that TTE is primarily governed by aggregate load power, nominal capacity, and open-circuit voltage, while electrochemical shape parameters contribute marginally to runtime variability. Temperature and aging effects are non-negligible: TTE peaks near room temperature and decreases significantly with cycle count as usable capacity declines.

The proposed framework offers an interpretable and computationally efficient tool for runtime estimation, battery-aware scheduling, and power-management policy design. The methodology is also transferable to other battery-powered platforms, including tablets, wearables, and electric mobility systems.
