                                            Summary

      Rapid battery drain in smartphones remains a critical issue, as battery behavior is difficult
to predict due to complex electrochemical processes and dynamic usage loads. To address this
challenge, we develop an integrated continuous-time mathematical model that combines elec-
trochemical principles with power decomposition to accurately simulate State of Charge (SOC)
dynamics and predict Time-to-Empty (TTE) under realistic usage conditions.
     Our framework begins by establishing and comparing candidate voltage-SOC models to select
an accurate core formulation, then systematically enhances it with temperature and aging dynamics.
Concurrently, we decompose the total device power into five constituent loads (screen, SoC, radio,
GPS, baseline). The parameters were calibrated based on the XJTU and NASA datasets.
      For Requirement 1: We develope two voltage-SOC models to simulate battery discharge: a
parsimonious Nernst-Based Model (NBM) for baseline accuracy, and a comprehensive Shepherd-
Based Model, which captures polarization, ohmic drop, and transient behavior, achieving a 75.5%
RMSE improvement. The framework further incorporates temperature dependence via Arrhenius
relationships and models battery aging through cycle-dependent capacity fade and resistance
growth.
     For Requirement 2: We compute Time-to-Empty (TTE) predictions under five representative
usage scenarios: Standby (22.69 h), Navigation (9.07 h), Gaming (3.84 h), Video Streaming (6.42 h),
and Reading (9.69 h). The results show that Gaming exhibits the shortest TTE due to simultaneous
high utilization of SoC, screen, and radio components, while Radio subsystem emerges as the
dominant power driver in streaming scenarios, exceeding 40% of total consumption
      For Requirement 3: We conduct a comprehensive sensitivity and assumptions analysis. The
predictions are most sensitive to two key modeling assumptions: ambient temperature (causing
TTE to vary non-linearly, peaking near 25°C) and battery aging (reducing effective capacity to 88%
after 500 cycles). A local parameter sensitivity analysis (±5% perturbation) confirms that TTE is
primarily governed by total power, nominal capacity , and open-circuit voltage, while electrochem-
ical shape parameters have a negligible impact (<0.3% change in TTE). Furthermore, fluctuations
in usage patterns—specifically, the order of activities and background applications—can alter
TTE by up to 3.76% and reduce runtime by 16.87%, respectively.
     For Requirement 4: We derive practical recommendations for users: maintain optimal SOC
range (20%–80%) to extend battery lifespan, minimize unnecessary GPS and notification permis-
sions, and avoid prolonged high-power operation to mitigate thermal aging. For OS developers,
we recommend implementing signal-aware radio power scaling, predictive background task batch-
ing, and health-aware power budgeting. This modeling framework is also generalizable to other
battery-powered devices like tablets and electric vehicles.

     Keywords: Shepherd Model, State of Charge, Time-to-Empty, Battery Aging, Power Decom-
position, Parameter-Temperature Response, Nernst Equation

1     Introduction
      Over the past few decades, smartphone battery drain has become an increasingly critical
concern for billions of users worldwide. There is the challenge of managing power consumption
while maintaining device performance. Then too, in addition to immediate usage factors such
as screen brightness and processor load, new complexities like battery aging and temperature
effects have begun to contribute to the unpredictability of battery behavior. Additionally, with
the proliferation of always-on connectivity including 4G/5G, WiFi, and Bluetooth, understanding
power consumption patterns has become essential for both users and system designers.
      Response to these challenges has been varied. Since the early development of lithium-ion
batteries, researchers have invested in various modeling approaches such as equivalent circuit
models and electrochemical models [2, 10]. In response to the need for accurate power profiling,
previous studies have characterized power consumption patterns in mobile devices [8, 7, 17],
revealing that screen, radio modules, and system-on-chip (SoC) dominate energy usage under
various scenarios. Recent work has extended these analyses to modern network technologies
including LTE [14] and 5G [21], while also addressing WiFi and Bluetooth impacts [19, 18].
      Given the critical importance of battery life in modern smartphones, we wish to investi-
gate what the optimal modeling approaches and prediction methods might be. To this end, we
seek to develop a continuous-time mathematical model that takes into consideration the behav-
ior of electrochemical dynamics, the component-level power decomposition, and the effects on
battery performance resulting from aging and temperature variations. Through this model, we
can then predict the time-to-empty under various usage scenarios, which can then inform prac-
tical recommendations for users and operating system developers, and even be extended to other
battery-powered devices.
      Our task is to develop a continuous-time mathematical model of a smartphone’s battery that
returns the state of charge (SOC) as a function of time under realistic usage conditions. This will
be used to predict the remaining time-to-empty under different conditions. Specifically, we need to
address:

    1. Continuous-Time Model: Develop a model to represent the state of charge using a continuous-
       time equation or system of equations, incorporating contributors such as screen usage, pro-
       cessor load, network connections, GPS usage, and other background tasks.

    2. Time-to-Empty Predictions: Use the model to compute or approximate the time-to-empty
       under various initial charge levels and usage scenarios, identifying the specific drivers of
       rapid battery drain.

    3. Sensitivity and Assumptions: Examine how predictions vary after making changes in
       modeling assumptions, parameter values, and fluctuations in usage patterns.

    4. Recommendations: Translate findings into practical recommendations for cellphone users
       and operating system power-saving strategies, considering how battery aging reduces effective
       capacity.

2.1   Battery
B-1 The batteries under investigation in this study are lithium-ion polymer batteries, which exhibit
    reversible electrochemical reactions.

B-2 Given that the side reactions during a single discharge cycle contribute negligibly, the elec-
    trochemical process can be simplified as the reaction between lithium ions and the electrodes.

B-3 The electrode features a porous structure, and the battery’s charge and discharge reactions
    involve the intercalation and deintercalation of lithium ions within the solid materials of the
    positive and negative electrodes, as shown in the figure below.




                              Figure 2: Battery structure schematic

B-4 The total amount of electrochemically active material in the anode and cathode is assumed
    to be comparable. Polarization losses are assumed to vary linearly with the current density
    in the active material, while the electrolyte resistance is treated as constant during discharge.

B-5 At equilibrium, the number of lithium ions occupying sites in the electrode material corre-
    sponds to the battery’s capacity.

2.2   Power consumption
 P-1 The total power consumption of a mobile phone is assumed to consist primarily of the fol-
     lowing components: screen, system-on-chip (SoC), radio modules, GPS, and baseline power
     consumption [8, 7]. The contributions of other components are assumed to be negligible.

 P-2 Only liquid crystal displays (LCDs) are considered. Their power consumption is assumed to
     originate from the backlight unit and auxiliary modulation circuitry [16]. When the screen
     is off, these components are assumed to be inactive.

 P-3 Radio power consumption refers to the energy consumption associated with WiFi, cellular
     data transmission (4G/5G), and Bluetooth communication [14, 21, 19, 18].

 P-4 It is assumed that the radio and GPS modules only draw significant power during initial
     activation, and once operational, they maintain stable signal output with constant steady-state
     power consumption.

 P-5 Baseline power consumption is defined as the baseline power required to maintain the phone
     in an operational state. It is assumed to be time-invariant and to persist regardless of other
     concurrent activities.

2.3   Temperature
 T-1 The ambient temperature is assumed to lie within the range of 0–50 ◦ C, which covers typical
     indoor and outdoor operating conditions for smartphones [1].

 T-2 The temperature of the phone is assumed to remain constant over time, as the thermal
     relaxation time is considered to be much shorter than the battery discharge time.

 T-3 Due to the relatively high thermal conductivity of the metallic components, the temperature
     within the phone—excluding the SoC module—is assumed to be spatially uniform. The SoC
     module, which generates a localized hot zone under operational load, is also treated as having
     a uniform internal temperature distribution [5, 4].

2.4   Aging
A-1 Battery aging is a long-term process and is not considered during a single discharge cycle.

A-2 Battery aging is assumed to be caused by two independent mechanisms: cycle aging (due to
    charge-discharge cycling) and calendar aging (due to storage time). The total capacity loss
    is the sum of contributions from both mechanisms [20, 23].
Team 2600101                                    Problem A                                    Page 5


 A-3 The capacity fade is assumed to be linearly correlated with the increase in internal resistance,
     consistent with the dominant role of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer growth in both
     phenomena [15, 13].

 A-4 The internal resistance is assumed to grow as a power-law function of the equivalent full
     cycle number, with the exponent 𝛾 ranging between 0.5 and 2 [13].

 A-5 Calendar aging is assumed to follow a square-root-of-time dependence, reflecting the diffusion-
     limited growth kinetics of the SEI layer [20].

 A-6 The aging rate is assumed to follow Arrhenius temperature dependence, with higher temper-
     atures accelerating degradation [3, 11].

 A-7 Aging is assumed to be accelerated at high state of charge (SOC), particularly above 75%,
     due to increased electrode reactivity [20].

2.5    Other assumptions
 O-1 Contact resistance and wire resistance are neglected.

 O-2 Self-discharge during typical usage periods (hours to days) is assumed to be negligible
     compared to load-driven discharge.

6.4    Drivers of Rapid Battery Drain
      The model quantitatively identifies several key factors influencing battery life. Activities
that produce the greatest reductions in runtime include high simultaneous loads, as exemplified
by the Gaming scenario, where the combined utilization of SoC, radio, and a high-refresh-rate
screen generates the highest total power, resulting in the shortest TTE (3.84 h, Table 5). Similarly,
sustained high-power radio usage, as in the Video Streaming scenario, dominates overall energy
consumption and leads to a substantial decrease in TTE (6.42 h).
      In contrast, certain conditions have surprisingly little effect on battery life. When the screen
is disabled, as in the Standby scenario, total power consumption is minimal, allowing the battery to
last the longest (22.69 h). Background computational tasks, modeled as a 10% offset to SoC load,
have only a minor impact on TTE in high-power scenarios, though they are more noticeable in low-
power situations such as Standby. Isolated use of GPS or moderate screen brightness also results
in relatively small reductions in battery life compared with the pronounced effects of combined
component loads in Gaming and Video Streaming.
      These findings demonstrate that the greatest drivers of rapid battery drain arise from concurrent
high-demand component usage, while isolated or low-power activities change the battery runtime
surprisingly little.

8     Recommendations and Practical Implications
8.1    User Behavior Recommendations
     Based on the modeling results and sensitivity analysis presented in previous sections, we
derive the following practical recommendations for smartphone users to optimize battery longevity
and daily runtime.

    1. Maintain optimal SOC range (20%–80%): As demonstrated by our aging model (Equa-
       tion 26), the SOC acceleration factor 𝑓 (SOC) = exp(𝜆 · SOC) indicates that storage at high
       SOC significantly accelerates capacity degradation. Avoiding prolonged periods at 100% or
       near 0% SOC can reduce SEI layer growth and extend battery lifespan by up to 30%.

    2. Minimize unnecessary GPS and notification permissions: Our component power analysis
       shows that GPS operates at 100% utilization during navigation scenarios. Restricting GPS
       access to essential applications and disabling push notifications for non-critical apps can
       reduce background power consumption by 15–25%.

    3. Avoid prolonged high-power operation: The Arrhenius temperature dependence (Equa-
       tion 6) demonstrates that elevated temperatures exponentially accelerate aging. Intensive
       gaming or video streaming sessions generate significant heat; limiting continuous high-load
       operation to 30–45 minute intervals with cooling breaks can substantially mitigate thermal
       aging.
Team 2600101                                Problem A                                        Page 20


  4. Monitor battery health indicators: For batteries exceeding 300–400 charge cycles, the
     combined effects of capacity fade (𝛼𝑄 ·𝑁 𝛾 ) and resistance growth (𝛽 𝑅 ·𝑁 𝛾 ) become noticeable.
     Users should expect 15–25% reduced runtime and consider replacement when capacity falls
     below 80% of nominal.

8.2   Operating System Power Management Strategies
     Developers of modern mobile operating systems can leverage insights from our model to
implement intelligent power management strategies that balance user experience with battery
preservation.

  1. Signal-aware radio power scaling: Implement adaptive radio transmit power based on
     received signal strength indicators (RSSI). In weak signal conditions, rather than continuously
     boosting transmission power, the OS should batch data transfers or suggest WiFi alternatives
     to users.

  2. Predictive background task batching: Group background synchronization tasks (email,
     social media, cloud backup) into periodic bursts rather than continuous polling. This reduces
     the frequency of radio module activation and allows longer deep-sleep intervals.

  3. Age-aware power budgeting: For aged batteries with elevated internal resistance 𝑅(𝑁), the
     OS should proactively reduce maximum brightness and CPU frequency to maintain stable
     voltage output and prevent premature shutdown at low SOC.

  4. Smart charging algorithms: Implement charging protocols that slow charge rate above
     80% SOC and provide options to cap maximum charge level at 80–85% for users prioritizing
     battery longevity over daily runtime.

8.3   Model Extensions: Generalization to Other Devices
    The proposed framework naturally extends to other battery-powered devices by adapting
component power coefficients and aging parameters.

  1. Tablets: Scale screen power proportionally to display area; reduce radio power contribution
     due to typically WiFi-dominant usage patterns.

  2. Wearables: Emphasize idle power and sensor (heart rate, accelerometer) contributions;
     battery capacities are typically 200–500 mAh with correspondingly scaled parameters.

  3. Electric vehicles: The aging submodel provides a foundation for battery health estimation
     systems. The capacity fade model (Equation 25) and cycle-dependent degradation can be
     scaled to automotive battery packs with appropriate cell-level aggregation.

  4. IoT devices: For always-on sensors with intermittent connectivity, the radio power model
     becomes critical; duty cycling optimization directly applies our power decomposition frame-
     work.

9     Strengths and Limitations
9.1   Strengths
 S-1 Physically meaningful Shepherd model with rigorous theoretical basis: The adopted
     Shepherd model combines theoretical rigor with clear physical interpretation. Each term
     in the model corresponds to a distinct electrochemical phenomenon: the polarization term
     (𝐾/SOC· 𝐼) captures concentration-dependent overpotential, the ohmic term (𝑅 · 𝐼) represents
     resistive losses, and the exponential term models initial transient behavior. This physically
     grounded formulation ensures interpretable parameters and reliable predictions under varying
     operating conditions.

 S-2 Comprehensive component-level power decomposition: The explicit separation of power
     consumption into screen, SoC, radio (4G/5G, WiFi, Bluetooth), GPS, and baseline compo-
     nents enables precise identification of dominant power drivers. This decomposition directly
     supports targeted optimization strategies for both users and OS designers.

 S-3 Integrated aging dynamics capturing history dependence: The aging submodel incor-
     porates both cycle aging (power-law with equivalent full cycles 𝑁 𝛾 ) and calendar aging
                          √
     (square-root-of-time 𝑡 𝑠 ), along with Arrhenius temperature dependence and SOC acceler-
     ation factors. This unified formulation addresses the problem statement’s emphasis on how
     “a battery’s behavior is influenced by its history.”

 S-4 Dual-model comparative framework: The parallel development of both Nernst-Based
     Model (NBM) and Shepherd model provides different perspectives on battery behavior—
     NBM offering fundamental electrochemical interpretation while the Shepherd model excels
     in practical discharge prediction. The systematic comparison between these two distinct
     approaches validates our model selection and enhances understanding of their respective
     applicability domains.

 S-5 Validated against real-world datasets: Rigorous validation using XJTU and Kaggle battery
     datasets demonstrates superior predictive accuracy, with voltage RMSE below 50 mV and
     discharge time prediction errors within 3% of experimental observations.

 S-6 Actionable practical recommendations: The model directly translates into quantitative
     guidance for users (optimal SOC range, brightness management, GPS restriction) and OS
     developers (adaptive DVFS, signal-aware radio scaling, age-aware power budgeting).

9.2   Limitations
L-1 Temperature treated as quasi-static: While temperature effects on capacity (𝑄 0 (𝑇)) and
    resistance (𝑅(𝑇)) are modeled via Arrhenius relationships, the thermal dynamics (heat gen-
    eration, dissipation, spatial gradients) are not explicitly coupled with the electrical model.
    This limits accuracy during rapid thermal transients such as gaming bursts.

L-2 Homogeneous aging assumption: The model assumes uniform degradation across all cells
    and electrode regions. In reality, manufacturing variations and localized thermal hotspots
    cause heterogeneous aging that may induce early cell-to-cell imbalance in multi-cell config-
    urations.
Team 2600101                                 Problem A                                       Page 22


 L-3 Simplified DVFS representation: The DVFS extension models steady-state power–frequency
     relationships, omitting transient switching losses and frequency governor dynamics that affect
     real-time power consumption during rapid workload changes.

 L-4 Constant-power scenario approximation: Within each usage scenario, power consumption
     is treated as constant. Realistic usage involves stochastic load fluctuations (variable video
     bitrates, intermittent GPS fixes) that introduce prediction uncertainty not captured in the
     deterministic model.

 L-5 Self-discharge neglected: For extended standby periods exceeding several days, self-
     discharge mechanisms (estimated at 2–5% per month for Li-ion cells) may become non-
     negligible and should be incorporated for long-term predictions.

10     Conclusion
      We model smartphone battery drain using several approaches. Rather than having a single,
prescriptive metric, we come up with several which correspond to battery behavior in different
ways. We then select a subset of these predictors through systematic model comparison.
      By using the Nernst-Based Model (NBM) as a simple yet effective representation of elec-
trochemical thermodynamics, we are able to condense the information about the voltage–SOC
relationship into interpretable parameters, achieving reasonable accuracy with minimal complex-
ity. Building upon this foundation, we develop the Shepherd model which provides comprehensive
modeling of battery dynamics and achieves highly accurate predictions, capturing voltage plateau
characteristics and current-dependent polarization with superior precision.
      Using an innovative physics-based framework, we are able to predict how long a battery will
last under various usage conditions, how battery aging affects performance over time, and how
temperature influences discharge behavior. The innovative aspect of the model is that it is made
up of three submodels (voltage dynamics, power decomposition, and aging effects) which, when
conditioned upon the data, are independent. Hence the overall model can be quite large while still
being efficient enough to perform numerical integration and obtain predictions from the posterior
distribution. Given the physics-based nature of the model, it is also able to provide uncertainties on
any predictions made in the form of parameter sensitivities instead of simple point estimates. We
provide several predictions supposing representative usage scenarios and provide several validations
to confirm that our model aligns with experimental data.
