\noindent Dear Editor,

Please consider the enclosed manuscript entitled \textbf{``A Unified Continuous-Time Electrochemical--Power Coupled Framework for Portable Energy Storage Management and Time-to-Empty Prediction''} that we are submitting for its eventual publication in \emph{Energy Conversion and Management} (EC\&M) as a research paper.

The core contribution is a \emph{unified} framework---not merely a collection of known submodels, but a coupled differential-algebraic system whose multiplicative temperature--aging interaction produces non-additive TTE effects that no prior single-model study captures.
Specifically, the paper couples a modified Shepherd electrochemical voltage model with a five-component hardware power disaggregation, logistic--Arrhenius temperature response, and power-law cycle/calendar aging to predict the time-to-empty (TTE) of lithium-ion cells in portable energy storage systems.
Unlike the electrothermal-aging charging model of Liu et al.\ (2019, EC\&M), our framework targets the \emph{discharge} side, provides \emph{component-level} load attribution for targeted subsystem throttling, and uses an electrochemically interpretable Shepherd voltage core (5 parameters) rather than abstract RC circuits.
Cross-batch generalisation (2\,C$\to$3\,C, 15 cells, no re-fitting) confirms transferability across C-rates---a robustness test absent from prior work.
Additionally, dynamic-load validation on the NASA Randomized Battery Usage Dataset (random-walk currents spanning 0.5--4\,A) yields a voltage RMSE of 63.5\,mV across 200 cycles using parameters identified solely from constant-current data, demonstrating that the Shepherd formulation transfers to time-varying loads without re-identification.
The proposed formulation achieves a voltage-tracking RMSE of 17.85\,mV, reducing error by over 55\% relative to Th\'{e}venin-1RC and 75\% relative to a Nernst baseline.
A receding-horizon model-predictive control (MPC) energy management strategy extends simulated runtime by up to 58\% (TTE = 6.0\,h vs.\ 3.8\,h baseline) with 56\% energy-efficiency improvement, outperforming rule-based thresholds by 18 percentage points while producing a smooth, continuous power trajectory.

We believe that the paper is appropriate for EC\&M because it addresses the system-level energy conversion chain---from electrochemical state evolution to workload-adaptive power delivery---and proposes an optimised energy management strategy that demonstrably extends device runtime through model-predictive throttling.
The framework's computational cost of 0.009\,ms per simulated second enables real-time deployment on embedded hardware, bridging the gap between high-fidelity battery modelling and practical energy management on resource-constrained platforms.
These relevant issues perfectly fit within the scope of the journal, and we are confident that the paper meets the expectations of EC\&M.

This manuscript has not been published previously and is not under consideration elsewhere.
