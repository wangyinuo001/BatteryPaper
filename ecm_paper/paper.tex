\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a simulation and modelling framework, validated at the electrochemical level, for energy conversion and management in portable electronic systems.
The framework couples four submodels---a modified Shepherd voltage formulation, a five-component power decomposition, logistic capacity--temperature and Arrhenius resistance--temperature coupling, and power-law cycle/calendar aging---into a unified continuous-time differential-algebraic system that predicts the time-to-empty (TTE) and enables physics-informed adaptive power management.
Electrochemical parameters are identified from the XJTU lithium-ion battery dataset and validated against four established voltage models; the proposed formulation achieves a voltage-tracking RMSE of \SI{17.85}{\milli\volt}, reducing error by over 55\% relative to Th\'{e}venin-1RC and 75\% relative to a Nernst baseline on constant-current discharge data.
Cross-batch validation without re-fitting confirms generalisability across C-rates, and dynamic-load validation on the NASA random-walk dataset (currents spanning 0.5--\SI{4}{\ampere}) yields a voltage RMSE of \SI{63.5}{\milli\volt} across 200 cycles using parameters identified solely from constant-current data.
Component-level load profiles are constructed from published hardware power measurements and applied as inputs to the validated electrochemical core, enabling scenario-based TTE exploration under diverse usage patterns.
Systematic ablation across a temperature--aging grid quantifies each module's contribution, with temperature coupling (up to 70\% TTE error) and aging dynamics (up to 55\%) dominating prediction accuracy.
A receding-horizon model-predictive control (MPC) energy management strategy extends simulated runtime by up to 58\% under high-intensity workloads (TTE $= \SI{6.0}{\hour}$ vs.\ $\SI{3.8}{\hour}$ baseline), with energy-efficiency improvement of approximately \SI{56}{\percent}.
The framework's computational cost of \SI{0.009}{\milli\second} per simulated second enables real-time deployment on embedded hardware and low-power microcontrollers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
Efficient energy conversion and management in portable electronic systems has become a critical challenge as the global fleet of lithium-ion-powered devices surpasses six billion units~\cite{Benkhelifa2016}.
Unlike stationary storage or electric-vehicle batteries, portable-device cells operate under highly dynamic and heterogeneous loads---screen brightness, system-on-chip (SoC)\footnote{Throughout this paper, ``SOC'' (all capitals) denotes state of charge, while ``SoC'' (mixed case) refers to the system-on-chip hardware component.} utilisation, radio-frequency communication, GPS positioning, and background services jointly determine the instantaneous power draw~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014}.
Accurate prediction of the \emph{time-to-empty} (TTE)---the interval from the current state of charge (SOC) to a cutoff threshold---directly underpins adaptive power management, health-aware charge scheduling, and system-level energy efficiency optimisation~\cite{Benkhelifa2016,Liu2019ECM}.
From an energy-management perspective, the ability to forecast remaining runtime under heterogeneous loads---accounting for temperature-dependent capacity, aging-induced degradation, and component-level power disaggregation---enables model-predictive throttling strategies that reduce wasteful energy conversion and extend the useful service life of the cell~\cite{Liu2019ECM,Liu2018ECM}.

Existing battery modelling approaches span a wide spectrum.
At one extreme, physics-based electrochemical models (e.g., pseudo-two-dimensional models) offer mechanistic fidelity but demand extensive parameterisation and prohibitive computation for real-time deployment~\cite{Doyle1993,Newman2004,Mama2025}.
At the other, data-driven methods (neural networks, Gaussian processes) bypass physical insight and require large labelled datasets that are rarely available under diverse operating conditions~\cite{Chemali2018,Richardson2017,Xu2012ECM}.
Equivalent-circuit models (ECMs)---including Rint, first-order Th\'{e}venin, and second-order Th\'{e}venin networks---occupy a middle ground with modest computational cost, yet they parameterise voltage dynamics via abstract RC elements that lack direct electrochemical interpretability~\cite{Hu2012,You2018,He2012ECM}.
The classical Shepherd formulation~\cite{Shepherd1965} offers explicit terms for polarisation drop, ohmic loss, and initial transient behaviour with clear physical correspondence, but has been underexplored for portable-device TTE prediction that must simultaneously account for component-level power disaggregation, temperature coupling, and long-term aging.
Fuller~\cite{Fuller2014} demonstrated a Shepherd-type model for constant-power discharge with rate effects, yet without multi-component load coupling, temperature--aging dynamics, or energy management integration that modern portable systems demand.
Specifically, Fuller's model (i)~targets primary alkaline cells (non-rechargeable), whereas portable devices universally employ rechargeable lithium-ion chemistries with distinct voltage profiles and aging mechanisms; (ii)~incorporates Peukert-type rate derating for variable-current discharge but assumes isothermal conditions, omitting the Arrhenius-governed resistance increase and logistic capacity reduction that dominate lithium-ion performance variation across the $0$--$45\,^{\circ}$C operating range; (iii)~treats the load as a scalar constant power, without component-level disaggregation that would enable targeted subsystem throttling; and (iv)~does not model cycle or calendar aging, making it unsuitable for lifetime-aware energy management.
The present work retains Fuller's core advantage---physical transparency of the Shepherd voltage equation---while addressing all four limitations through the coupled temperature, aging, and component-level power submodels developed in \secref{sec:model}.

Several studies have characterised smartphone power consumption at the component level~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014,Murmuria2012}, revealing that screen, SoC, and radio subsystems dominate energy usage.
Recent work has extended these analyses to 5G and WiFi power profiling~\cite{Xu2020,Lauridsen2014,Sun2018,Negri2006}, but the coupling between load decomposition and electrochemical state evolution remains largely ad hoc.
On the aging front, capacity fade and resistance growth in lithium-ion cells have been modelled via power-law cycling degradation and Arrhenius-accelerated calendar aging~\cite{Vetter2005,Benson2012,Li2019,Kou2017}, while temperature effects on capacity and internal resistance are well characterised~\cite{Ali2020,Chen2025,He2017,Tang2019ECM}.
Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2019ECM} addressed battery charging management considering degradation costs, and Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2018ECM} optimised multi-objective charging patterns---both within the energy conversion and management community---yet neither integrates real-time TTE prediction under heterogeneous loads.

Despite the maturity of individual submodels, their joint integration into a unified continuous-time TTE framework with component-level power coupling and experimentally validated cross-condition generalisation remains largely unexplored~\cite{Tian2019,Xiong2018,Mama2025}.
Critically, this gap is not merely one of implementation convenience: the coupling itself produces \emph{non-additive} effects---for example, the interaction between temperature-reduced capacity and aging-induced resistance growth yields TTE deviations that cannot be predicted by either submodel in isolation (see the ablation study in \secref{sec:ablation}).
This paper addresses the gap with the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Unified continuous-time simulation framework.}
    To our knowledge, this is the first framework to embed all four submodels---an electrochemically interpretable voltage formulation, hardware-component-level power disaggregation, logistic--Arrhenius temperature response, and power-law cycle/calendar aging---into a single coupled ODE system with a closed-form TTE computation (\secref{sec:model}).
    The electrochemical parameters are identified and validated on real cell data from the XJTU dataset~\cite{XJTU2020}; component-level load profiles are constructed from published smartphone hardware power measurements~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014,Lu2008} and applied as synthetic inputs to the validated electrochemical core.
    This decoupled validation approach---electrochemistry on real cell data, loads from literature---enables systematic scenario exploration while maintaining a sound electrochemical foundation (see \secref{sec:datasets} for a detailed discussion of this methodology and its limitations).
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive baseline comparison and ablation.}
    Against four established voltage models (Nernst-based, Rint, Th\'{e}venin-1RC, Th\'{e}venin-2RC), the proposed framework achieves the lowest voltage-tracking RMSE (\SI{17.85}{\milli\volt}) under constant-current discharge conditions.
    A systematic ablation study across a $3\times3$ temperature--aging grid quantifies the marginal contribution of each submodel to TTE accuracy and, crucially, reveals that the coupling is non-trivially synergistic: the combined temperature--aging TTE error is not the sum of the individual ablation errors, demonstrating interaction effects that justify the unified formulation (\secref{sec:ablation}).
    \item \textbf{Cross-batch generalisation and statistical validation.}
    Parameters fitted on XJTU Batch-1 (2\,C rate) are directly applied to Batch-2 (3\,C rate) without re-fitting, demonstrating transferability.
    Bootstrap confidence intervals confirm that all reported metrics are statistically robust (\secref{sec:crossbatch}).
    \item \textbf{Model-predictive energy management.}
    We formulate discharge-side power scheduling as a receding-horizon MPC problem (\eqnrefp{eq:mpc}) with SOC-tracking and quality-of-service objectives, using the Shepherd model as the internal prediction engine.  The MPC extends simulated runtime by up to \SI{58}{\percent} (TTE $= \SI{6.0}{\hour}$ vs.\ $\SI{3.8}{\hour}$ baseline) and improves energy efficiency by \SI{56}{\percent}, outperforming a rule-based threshold strategy ($+40\%$) while producing a smooth, continuous power trajectory---a qualitative advantage over the abrupt mode switches common in commercial OSes.
    A computational cost of \SI{0.009}{\milli\second} per simulated second (\tabref{tab:efficiency}) confirms suitability for embedded deployment on low-power microcontrollers (\secref{sec:energy_mgmt}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
Battery modelling approaches span a wide spectrum of complexity and interpretability.
Physics-based electrochemical models (e.g., the pseudo-two-dimensional Doyle--Fuller--Newman formulation~\cite{Doyle1993,Newman2004}) offer mechanistic fidelity but require extensive parameterisation and prohibitive computation for real-time deployment~\cite{Mama2025}.
Data-driven methods (neural networks, Gaussian processes) bypass physical insight and demand large labelled datasets rarely available under diverse operating conditions~\cite{Chemali2018,Richardson2017,Xu2012ECM,Lipu2021}.
Equivalent-circuit models (ECMs)---including Rint, Th\'{e}venin-1RC, and 2RC networks---enjoy widespread use in battery management systems owing to their low computational cost, but their parameters lack direct electrochemical meaning~\cite{Hu2012,You2018,He2012ECM,Birkl2015}.
The classical Shepherd formulation~\cite{Shepherd1965} decomposes terminal voltage into physically transparent terms (OCV, polarisation, ohmic drop, exponential transient), yet has been underexplored for portable-device TTE prediction.
Fuller~\cite{Fuller2014} demonstrated a Shepherd-type model for constant-power discharge of primary alkaline cells, incorporating Peukert-type rate effects to account for reduced effective capacity at high discharge currents.
While Fuller's model provides an instructive precedent for analytically tractable Shepherd-based runtime prediction, it assumes isothermal operation, treats the load as a scalar constant power without component decomposition, and does not model aging---assumptions that must be relaxed for rechargeable lithium-ion cells in portable devices.

On the load side, Carroll and Heiser~\cite{Carroll2010} and Chen et al.~\cite{Chen2014} provided comprehensive smartphone power breakdowns, identifying screen, SoC, and radio as dominant consumers.
Subsequent work characterised LTE~\cite{Lauridsen2014}, 5G~\cite{Xu2020}, WiFi~\cite{Sun2018}, and Bluetooth~\cite{Negri2006} profiles.
However, these studies report measurements in isolation, without coupling to electrochemical state evolution.
On the degradation front, temperature effects on capacity and resistance are well characterised via logistic and Arrhenius models~\cite{Ali2020,Chen2025,He2017,Benson2012,Tang2019ECM}, while cycle aging follows power-law relations~\cite{Vetter2005,Kou2017,Li2019} and calendar aging exhibits $\sqrt{t}$ dependence~\cite{Vetter2005}.
Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2019ECM} proposed a coupled electrothermal-aging model for economy-conscious battery charging management that jointly captures electrical, thermal, and degradation dynamics across different timescales using a second-order RC circuit, a lumped thermal model, and a power-law capacity-loss submodel.
Their multi-timescale coupling architecture---fast electrical dynamics within a single charging cycle, slow aging dynamics across cycles---is a direct methodological precursor to the present work.
However, Liu et al.'s framework is designed for the \emph{charging} side in \emph{electric-vehicle} applications, and it does not address (a)~discharge-side TTE prediction, (b)~component-level load decomposition for heterogeneous portable-device workloads, or (c)~the use of electrochemically interpretable voltage models (Shepherd) in place of abstract RC circuits.
Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2018ECM} further optimised multi-objective charging patterns within the energy conversion and management community.
The present work extends this coupled-submodel philosophy from \emph{charging optimisation} to \emph{discharge-side energy management}, from \emph{EV-scale} to \emph{portable-device-scale} loads, and from \emph{ECM-based} to \emph{Shepherd-based} voltage dynamics with explicit physical interpretability.
Three specific technical advances beyond Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2019ECM} merit emphasis: (i)~the five-component power disaggregation enables \emph{targeted} load throttling (reducing a specific subsystem rather than blanket power reduction), (ii)~the Shepherd voltage core provides physically transparent parameter--phenomenon correspondence absent in RC-circuit representations, and (iii)~the cross-batch validation (2\,C$\to$3\,C, no re-fitting) directly tests and confirms the model's transferability across C-rates---a robustness test not reported in the prior work.
Despite the maturity of individual submodels, their joint integration into a portable-device TTE framework with experimentally validated cross-condition generalisation has not been demonstrated~\cite{Tian2019,Xiong2018,Mama2025,Yu2025}.

\section{Proposed Unified Continuous-Time Model}\label{sec:model}
This section develops the proposed framework from first principles.
The model comprises four coupled submodels---terminal-voltage dynamics (\ssref{sec:shepherd}), component-level power decomposition (\ssref{sec:power}), temperature--parameter coupling (\ssref{sec:temperature}), and aging dynamics (\ssref{sec:aging})---which are assembled into a unified differential-algebraic system (\ssref{sec:ode}) and solved numerically (\ssref{sec:numerics}).
\figref{fig:framework} illustrates the information flow among submodels.

\subsection{Terminal-voltage submodel}\label{sec:shepherd}
\subsubsection{Physical basis of the Shepherd formulation}
The terminal voltage of a lithium-ion cell during discharge is governed by three concurrent phenomena~\cite{Shepherd1965}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Thermodynamic equilibrium.} The open-circuit voltage~$E_0$ reflects the Gibbs free-energy difference between the electrode active materials at full charge.
    \item \emph{Mass-transport polarisation.} As discharge proceeds, lithium-ion depletion near the electrode surface creates a concentration gradient.  The resulting overpotential is proportional to the current~$I$ and inversely proportional to the remaining charge fraction~SOC, yielding a term~$K I/\mathrm{SOC}$.  Physically, $K$ (V$\cdot$s/Ah) encapsulates the effective diffusion resistance of the intercalation host.
    \item \emph{Ohmic loss and initial transient.}  Series resistance~$R$ produces an instantaneous $IR$ drop, while the exponential zone ($A$, $B$) captures the initial voltage relaxation associated with double-layer charging and surface-film effects during early discharge.
\end{enumerate}
Combining these contributions, the \emph{modified Shepherd equation} for terminal voltage reads
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shepherd}
V_{\mathrm{term}} = E_0 - \frac{K}{\mathrm{SOC}}\,I - R\,I + A\exp\!\bigl(-B Q_0(1-\mathrm{SOC})\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the four right-hand-side terms represent, from left to right, the open-circuit voltage, polarisation overpotential, ohmic drop, and exponential transient zone.
In the taxonomy of battery-scale electrical models surveyed by Mama et al.~\cite{Mama2025}, the Shepherd formulation occupies a distinct niche between purely empirical models and full equivalent-circuit networks: its coefficients are empirically determined---readily obtainable from standard discharge curves~\cite{Fuller2014}---yet each term retains a one-to-one mapping to an identifiable electrochemical phenomenon.
This combination of low parameterisation cost and physical transparency motivates its adoption as the voltage core of the present framework.

\subsubsection{SOC dynamics via Coulomb counting}
Conservation of charge relates the SOC evolution to the discharge current:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:coulomb}
\frac{\mathrm{d}\,\mathrm{SOC}}{\mathrm{d}t} = -\frac{I(t)}{Q_0},
\end{equation}
where $Q_0$ (Ah) is the effective cell capacity.  The sign convention is $I>0$ during discharge.

\subsubsection{Power-balance closure}
For a smartphone whose instantaneous total power consumption is $P_{\mathrm{total}}(t)$, the algebraic constraint
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power}
V_{\mathrm{term}}(\mathrm{SOC}, I) \cdot I = P_{\mathrm{total}}(t)
\end{equation}
closes the system by implicitly determining $I$ at each instant.

\subsubsection{Time-to-empty definition}
Given an initial condition $\mathrm{SOC}(0) = \mathrm{SOC}_0$, the TTE is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tte}
\mathrm{TTE} = \inf\bigl\{t > 0 \;\big|\; \mathrm{SOC}(t) \le \mathrm{SOC}_{\min} \;\text{or}\; V_{\mathrm{term}}(t) \le V_{\mathrm{cutoff}}\bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{SOC}_{\min} = 0.05$ and $V_{\mathrm{cutoff}} = \SI{2.5}{\volt}$ in this study.

\paragraph{Comparison with ECM representations.}
In a Th\'{e}venin-$n$RC model, the $n$ RC-voltage state variables evolve via linear ODEs whose time constants ($\tau_k = R_k C_k$) lack one-to-one correspondence with electrochemical processes.
The Shepherd form, by contrast, replaces these abstract dynamics with a single, physically transparent $K/\mathrm{SOC}$ polarisation term and an exponential transient zone, reducing the parameter count from $2n+1$ (Th\'{e}venin) to~5 while yielding superior accuracy on constant-rate discharge profiles (see \secref{sec:voltage}).

\subsection{Component-level power decomposition}\label{sec:power}
A smartphone's instantaneous power consumption is disaggregated into five hardware subsystems~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ptotal}
P_{\mathrm{total}} = P_{\mathrm{screen}} + P_{\mathrm{SoC}} + P_{\mathrm{radio}} + P_{\mathrm{GPS}} + P_{\mathrm{base}}.
\end{equation}
\paragraph{Screen power.}
OLED display power depends on luminance~$L$ (cd/m$^2$), active area~$A_s$ (m$^2$), and a refresh-rate scaling factor~$\eta$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:screen}
P_{\mathrm{screen}} = \eta\bigl(\gamma_s A_s L + \lambda_s A_s\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_s$ (W\,m$^{-2}$\,cd$^{-1}$) and $\lambda_s$ (W/m$^{2}$) are OLED-specific power coefficients calibrated from~\cite{Lu2008}, and
\begin{equation}
\eta = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{screen off,} \\ 1.0 & 60\,\text{Hz refresh,} \\ 1.3 & 120\,\text{Hz refresh.} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The factor $\eta = 1.3$ for 120\,Hz reflects the roughly 30\% increase in display driver power measured in recent high-refresh-rate panels.

\paragraph{Computational, communication, and baseline subsystems.}
Each remaining component's power is parameterised by a dimensionless \emph{working intensity}~$\xi_i \in [0,1]$, representing the fraction of its maximum rated power:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:xi}
\begin{pmatrix} P_{\mathrm{SoC}} \\ P_{\mathrm{radio}} \\ P_{\mathrm{base}} \end{pmatrix}
= \operatorname{diag}(\xi_1,\,\xi_2,\,\xi_3)
\begin{pmatrix} P_{\mathrm{SoC}}^{\max} \\ P_{\mathrm{radio}}^{\max} \\ P_{\mathrm{base}}^{\max} \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
GPS power is modelled as binary: $P_{\mathrm{GPS}} = P_{\mathrm{GPS}}^{\max}$ when satellite acquisition is active and zero otherwise, reflecting the all-or-nothing nature of positioning hardware.
Reference maximum powers adopted in this study are $P_{\mathrm{SoC}}^{\max} = \SI{2.04}{\watt}$, $P_{\mathrm{radio}}^{\max} = \SI{1.173}{\watt}$, $P_{\mathrm{GPS}}^{\max} = \SI{0.30}{\watt}$, and $P_{\mathrm{base}}^{\max} = \SI{0.12}{\watt}$, consistent with measurements reported in~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014}.
The SoC utilisation values in \tabref{tab:scenarios} include a 10\% baseline allocation for OS housekeeping processes.

\paragraph{Extensibility.}
This additive decomposition is modular: adding a new subsystem (e.g., NFC, UWB) amounts to appending a $(\xi_j, P_j^{\max})$ pair.

\subsection{Temperature--parameter coupling}\label{sec:temperature}
Temperature influences cell behaviour through three mechanisms, each modelled with a distinct functional form.
\subsubsection{Capacity--temperature response}
The maximum number of lithium ions accommodated in the electrode lattice follows a Fermi--Dirac (logistic) occupancy distribution.
Denoting by $Q_0'$ the limiting capacity at infinite temperature, the effective capacity at temperature~$T$ (in Kelvin) is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Q0T}
Q_0^{\mathrm{th}}(T) = \frac{Q_0'}{1 + \exp\!\Bigl(\alpha_\theta\bigl(\tfrac{1}{T} - \tfrac{1}{T_m}\bigr)\Bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_\theta$ (K) controls the steepness and $T_m$ (K) is the midpoint temperature.
Fitted values (see \secref{sec:paramfit}) are $\alpha_\theta = \SI{3000.0}{\kelvin}$ and $T_m = \SI{298.51}{\kelvin}$.
\emph{Physical interpretation.}
At low temperatures ($T \ll T_m$), the exponential term dominates, sharply reducing $Q_0^{\mathrm{th}}$.
This reflects the thermodynamic freezing-out of available lattice sites, consistent with experimental observations of reduced deliverable capacity below \SI{0}{\degreeCelsius}~\cite{Ali2020}.

\subsubsection{Resistance--temperature response}
The temperature dependence of the total internal resistance is governed by the Arrhenius law for thermally activated charge-transfer and ion-transport processes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:RT}
R^{\mathrm{th}}(T) = R_0\exp\!\biggl(\frac{E_r}{k_B}\Bigl(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_0}\Bigr)\biggr),
\end{equation}
where $E_r = \SI{0.005}{\electronvolt}$ is the effective activation energy, $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant, and $T_0 = \SI{298.15}{\kelvin}$ is the reference temperature~\cite{Benson2012,He2017,Jeon2011ECM}.
$R_0$ denotes the resistance at $T_0$.
The small value of $E_r$ indicates that temperature-induced resistance variation is modest ($<2\%$ over the operating range), with TTE sensitivity to temperature dominated instead by the logistic capacity response (\eqnrefp{eq:Q0T}).

\subsubsection{SoC leakage power}
At elevated temperatures, subthreshold leakage current in the SoC increases according to a MOSFET leakage model~\cite{Bhat2019,Bhat2021}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:leak}
P_{\mathrm{leak}}(T) = V_{\mathrm{nom}}\,c_1\,T^2\exp(c_2 / T),
\end{equation}
with $V_{\mathrm{nom}} = \SI{3.7}{\volt}$ (the nominal cell voltage), $c_1 = \SI{0.493}{\ampere\per\kelvin\squared}$, and $c_2 = \SI{-3740}{\kelvin}$.
This term is additive to the SoC dynamic power in \eqnref{eq:ptotal}.

\emph{Scope.}
Because $P_{\mathrm{leak}}(T_{\mathrm{ref}}) \approx 0$ at the \SI{25}{\degreeCelsius} reference, leakage has negligible impact on the nominal TTE results in \secref{sec:tte}.
It is therefore included only in the temperature-sweep analysis (\secref{sec:temp_tte}) where the high-temperature regime is explicitly explored, and omitted from the baseline TTE and ablation simulations to avoid introducing a temperature-dependent power offset that would obscure the comparison of electrochemical submodel contributions.

\subsection{Aging dynamics}\label{sec:aging}
Repeated charge--discharge cycling degrades cell performance through SEI layer growth, lithium plating, and active-material loss~\cite{Vetter2005}.
We model both \emph{cycle aging} and \emph{calendar aging}.
\subsubsection{Cycle aging: capacity fade and resistance growth}
Denoting by $N$ the number of equivalent full cycles, cycle-induced degradation is captured by power-law relations fitted to XJTU data:
\paragraph{Resistance growth.}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Raging}
R^{\mathrm{cyc}}(N) = R_0^{\mathrm{nom}}\bigl(1 + \beta_R\, N^{\gamma_R}\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $\beta_R = 1.229 \times 10^{-3}$ and $\gamma_R = 1.085$.  This super-linear growth ($\gamma_R > 1$) reflects SEI thickening and progressive pore clogging.
\paragraph{Capacity fade.}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Qaging}
Q_0^{\mathrm{cyc}}(N) = Q_0^{\mathrm{nom}}\bigl(1 - \alpha_Q\, N^{\gamma_Q}\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_Q = 2.56 \times 10^{-4}$ and $\gamma_Q = \gamma_R$.  Using a shared exponent $\gamma$ reflects the common mechanistic origin (SEI growth) underlying both degradation modes.

\subsubsection{Calendar aging}
During storage, diffusion-limited SEI growth produces a $\sqrt{t_s}$ capacity loss:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calaging}
\Delta Q_0^{\mathrm{cal}}(t_s, T, \mathrm{SOC}) = Q_0^{\mathrm{nom}}\,\kappa\,\sqrt{t_s}\,\exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{E_a}{R_g T} + \lambda_a\,\mathrm{SOC}\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\kappa = 10^{-5}$ (Ah$^{-1}$\,s$^{-1/2}$) is the calendar aging coefficient, $E_a = \SI{50}{\kilo\joule\per\mole}$ is the activation energy for SEI growth, $R_g = \SI{8.314}{\joule\per\mole\per\kelvin}$ is the universal gas constant, $\lambda_a = 1.5$ is the SOC-stress coefficient capturing the dependence on anode potential (higher SOC $\Rightarrow$ higher electrode potential $\Rightarrow$ faster parasitic reactions)~\cite{Vetter2005}.

\subsubsection{Combined aging--temperature coupling}\label{sec:coupling}
The effective capacity and resistance under simultaneous temperature and aging effects are obtained by composing the submodel outputs:
\begin{align}
Q_0(T, N, t_s) &= Q_0^{\mathrm{th}}(T) \cdot \frac{Q_0^{\mathrm{cyc}}(N) - \Delta Q_0^{\mathrm{cal}}}{Q_0^{\mathrm{nom}}}, \label{eq:Q0coupled}\\[4pt]
R(T, N) &= R^{\mathrm{th}}(T) \cdot \frac{R^{\mathrm{cyc}}(N)}{R_0^{\mathrm{nom}}}. \label{eq:Rcoupled}
\end{align}
\emph{Rationale.}
The multiplicative coupling in \eqnsref{eq:Q0coupled}{eq:Rcoupled} assumes that temperature and aging act on \emph{independent} physical mechanisms---thermal reduction of available lattice sites (\eqnrefp{eq:Q0T}) vs.\ electrochemical loss of active material (\refsref{eq:Qaging}{eq:calaging})---so their effects compose as scaling factors on the nominal values.
This separability assumption is common in the aging literature~\cite{Vetter2005,Li2019,Tang2019ECM} and is supported by the observation that temperature-dependent capacity tests at different cycle counts yield approximately parallel logistic curves (see XJTU temperature-compensation data).

\subsection{Coupled ODE system and TTE computation}\label{sec:ode}
Assembling the submodels, the complete state-evolution system is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:system}
\left\{\;
\begin{aligned}
& V_{\mathrm{term}} = E_0 - \frac{K}{\mathrm{SOC}}\,I - R(T,N)\,I + A\,\mathrm{e}^{-B\,Q_0(T,N)(1-\mathrm{SOC})}\\[6pt]
& \frac{\mathrm{d}\,\mathrm{SOC}}{\mathrm{d}t} = -\frac{I(t)}{Q_0(T,N)}\\[6pt]
& V_{\mathrm{term}}(I) \cdot I = P_{\mathrm{total}}(t)\\[4pt]
& \mathrm{SOC}(0) = \mathrm{SOC}_0
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}
This is a differential-algebraic equation (DAE) of index~1: the algebraic constraint (third line) can be solved for $I$ at each time step, after which the SOC ODE is integrated forward.
\paragraph{State vector.}
The minimal state is $\mathbf{x}(t) = [\mathrm{SOC}(t)]$ (one-dimensional), since temperature~$T$ and cycle count~$N$ are treated as slowly varying external parameters (quasi-static assumption).
\paragraph{Termination.}
Integration halts at $t^* = \mathrm{TTE}$ when $\mathrm{SOC}(t^*) \le 0.05$ or $V_{\mathrm{term}}(t^*) \le V_{\mathrm{cutoff}}$.

\subsection{Parameter identification}\label{sec:paramfit}
\subsubsection{Optimisation procedure}
The six Shepherd parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (E_0, K, R_0, A, B, Q_0^{\mathrm{nom}})$ are identified by minimising the voltage RMSE over the first discharge cycle of each Batch-1 cell:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obj}
\boldsymbol{\theta}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \sqrt{\frac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M}\bigl(V_{\mathrm{term}}^{\mathrm{pred}}(t_k;\boldsymbol{\theta}) - V_{\mathrm{meas}}(t_k)\bigr)^2},
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the number of time samples per discharge trajectory.
We use the \emph{differential evolution} (DE) global optimiser (SciPy, strategy ``best1bin'', population size 50, 1000 generations) with bound constraints reflecting physical plausibility (e.g., $E_0 \in [3.0, 4.5]$\,V, $R_0 \in [0.01, 0.2]$\,$\Omega$).

\subsubsection{Structural identifiability}
The Shepherd model has five independent algebraic terms, each involving a distinct functional dependence on $(I, \mathrm{SOC})$:
(i)~a constant ($E_0$),
(ii)~a term linear in $I$ ($R_0$),
(iii)~a term proportional to $I/\mathrm{SOC}$ ($K$),
(iv)~an exponential in $(1-\mathrm{SOC})$ ($A$, $B$).
Since a full discharge trajectory provides data spanning $\mathrm{SOC} \in [0.05, 1]$ with known $I(t)$, these four functional forms are linearly independent, ensuring \emph{structural identifiability} of all five parameters.
In practice, we verify \emph{practical identifiability} by running 20 independent DE optimisation trials with different random seeds.  The coefficient of variation of each fitted parameter is below 2\%, confirming that the global minimum is unique and robustly located.

\subsubsection{Aging-parameter estimation}
The aging coefficients $(\alpha_Q, \beta_R, \gamma)$ are fitted separately to the cycle-resolved capacity and resistance trajectories extracted from the XJTU dataset (Batch-1), using nonlinear least squares on \eqnsref{eq:Raging}{eq:Qaging}.

\subsection{Numerical solution}\label{sec:numerics}
\subsubsection{Algebraic current solver}
At each time step, the nonlinear algebraic equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rootfind}
f(I) \equiv V_{\mathrm{term}}(I, \mathrm{SOC}, T, N) \cdot I - P_{\mathrm{total}} = 0
\end{equation}
is solved for $I \ge 0$.
\begin{proposition}[Uniqueness of the physical root]\label{prop:mono}
Under the operating regime $E_0, K, R, A, B, Q_0 > 0$ and $\mathrm{SOC} \in (0, 1]$, let $F = E_0 + A\exp(-BQ_0(1-\mathrm{SOC}))$ and $\alpha = K/\mathrm{SOC} + R$.
The power function $g(I) = I\,V_{\mathrm{term}}(I) = FI - \alpha I^2$ is a concave quadratic with a unique maximum $g^* = F^2/(4\alpha)$.
For any $P_{\mathrm{total}} \in (0, g^*]$, \eqnref{eq:rootfind} has exactly one root on $(0, F/(2\alpha)]$, corresponding to the high-voltage (low-current) operating point.
\end{proposition}
The proof is given in \appref{app:proof}.
We implement a relaxed fixed-point iteration.
First, the initial guess is set as $I^{(0)} = P_{\mathrm{total}}/E_0$.
Second, the current is updated via $\tilde{I}^{(k+1)} = P_{\mathrm{total}} / V_{\mathrm{term}}(I^{(k)})$, followed by exponential-moving-average (EMA) smoothing $I^{(k+1)} = \alpha\, I^{(k)} + (1-\alpha)\,\tilde{I}^{(k+1)}$ with $\alpha = 0.9$.
Finally, under Proposition~\ref{prop:mono}'s monotonicity, the EMA iteration contracts to the unique physical root within $\sim$10 steps for all operating points encountered in this study.
Because the Shepherd voltage is a simple quadratic in $I$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:mono}), this lightweight scheme achieves the same converged current as a Newton--Raphson solver while avoiding derivative evaluation, and is well suited to embedded deployment.

\subsubsection{Time integration}
SOC is advanced by explicit Euler:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SOC}_{n+1} = \mathrm{SOC}_n - \frac{I_n}{Q_0}\,\Delta t,
\end{equation}
with a fixed step $\Delta t = \SI{1}{\second}$ chosen for reproducibility.
For stiff thermal--electrical couplings in future extensions, adaptive Runge--Kutta or implicit solvers (e.g., CVODE) are recommended.

\paragraph{Rationale for the Shepherd formulation over P2D.}
The computational advantage over physics-based models is not merely quantitative but reflects a fundamental trade-off surveyed across the multi-scale battery modelling literature~\cite{Mama2025}.
Pseudo-two-dimensional (P2D) models solve coupled partial differential equations for solid- and electrolyte-phase lithium transport, requiring spatial discretisation across electrodes and separator, temperature-dependent transport properties, and iterative nonlinear solvers at each time step~\cite{Doyle1993,Newman2004}.
While P2D models capture internal concentration gradients and electrode-level heterogeneity with high fidelity, they demand 50+ parameters (many measurable only through specialised electrochemical impedance spectroscopy or half-cell experiments) and wall-clock times on the order of minutes per discharge cycle even on workstation hardware~\cite{Mama2025}.
For the present application---real-time TTE estimation on an embedded microcontroller---such computational and parameterisation costs are prohibitive.
The Shepherd formulation achieves the essential voltage--SOC mapping with five parameters identifiable from a single constant-current discharge curve, at a computational cost four orders of magnitude lower (\tabref{tab:efficiency}), while sacrificing only the ability to resolve internal concentration profiles that are not directly observable in a portable-device BMS context.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:experiments}
\subsection{Datasets}\label{sec:datasets}
\paragraph{XJTU Battery Dataset.}
We use the publicly available XJTU lithium-ion battery dataset~\cite{XJTU2020}, comprising six batches of NMC/graphite 18650 cells.
Two batches are employed in this study.
Specifically, Batch-1 comprises 8 cells cycled at 2\,C constant-current discharge, serving as the \emph{fitting set} for parameter identification, while Batch-2 comprises 15 cells cycled at 3\,C constant-current discharge, used \emph{exclusively} for cross-batch generalisation testing.
Each cell record contains time-stamped voltage, current, temperature, and capacity measurements across hundreds of charge--discharge cycles.

\paragraph{Synthetic smartphone load profiles.}
The XJTU dataset provides cell-level electrochemical data under controlled cycling conditions (constant current) on a battery test bench.
No real-time smartphone hardware telemetry (e.g., concurrent screen brightness, CPU frequency, radio state) is available.
Therefore, the component-level power profiles used in the TTE simulations (\tabref{tab:scenarios}) are \emph{synthetic}: they are constructed from published per-component power measurements on real smartphone hardware~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014,Lu2008,Murmuria2012} and applied as inputs to the electrochemically parameterised model.
This approach decouples voltage-model validation (performed on real cell data) from load-profile specification (constructed from literature), enabling systematic exploration of usage scenarios that would be infeasible with purely empirical data collection.
The implications and limitations of this separation are discussed in \secref{sec:conclusion}.

\paragraph{NASA Randomized Battery Usage Dataset.}
To validate the Shepherd model under dynamic (non-constant-current) discharge conditions, we employ Dataset~\#11 from the NASA Prognostics Center of Excellence~\cite{Bole2014}.
This dataset comprises four 18650 lithium-ion cells (RW3--RW6, $\sim$\SI{2}{\ampere\hour}) subjected to two types of discharge:
(i)~\emph{reference CC discharges} at $\sim$\SI{1}{\ampere} to a \SI{3.2}{\volt} cutoff, recorded periodically as capacity benchmarks; and
(ii)~\emph{random-walk discharges}, where the current is drawn from a uniform distribution $I \sim \mathrm{U}(0.5, 4)$\,A and held constant for \SI{5}{\minute} segments until the cutoff is reached.
Each cell contains $\sim$44 reference discharges and $\sim$50 random-walk cycles.
This dataset is uniquely suited for testing the Shepherd model's transferability from CC-identified parameters to arbitrary current profiles, as the random-walk protocol spans a wide C-rate range ($0.25$--$2$C) within a single discharge.

\paragraph{Data preprocessing.}
Discharge segments are extracted by identifying contiguous intervals with $I > 0.01$\,A.
Voltage and current are downsampled to \SI{1}{\second} resolution via linear interpolation.
The reference SOC is obtained by Coulomb counting:
$\mathrm{SOC}(t) = 1 - \int_0^t I(\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\tau / Q_{\mathrm{meas}}$,
where $Q_{\mathrm{meas}}$ is the measured discharge capacity of the cycle.

\paragraph{Data split protocol.}
Batch-1 Cycle-1 data is used for parameter fitting; Batch-1 Cycles 2--$n$ are used for same-batch validation.
Batch-2 data is reserved entirely for cross-batch testing.
No Batch-2 data is used for fitting or hyperparameter selection.

\subsection{Baseline models}\label{sec:baselines}
We compare the proposed Shepherd-based framework against four voltage models spanning the interpretability--complexity spectrum:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Nernst-Based Model (NBM):}
    A thermodynamically motivated OCV model augmented with an exponential correction:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:nbm}
    V_{\mathrm{OC}} = E^0 + \frac{R_g T}{nF}\ln\!\frac{\mathrm{SOC}}{1-\mathrm{SOC}} + M\exp(-\alpha\cdot\mathrm{SOC}),
    \end{equation}
    with $V_{\mathrm{term}} = V_{\mathrm{OC}} - IR$.  Parameters: $E^0 = \SI{4.5}{\volt}$, $M = -1.1718$, $\alpha = 0.8022$, fitted by nonlinear least squares.
    \item \textbf{Rint (Internal Resistance):}
    $V = \mathrm{OCV}(\mathrm{SOC}) - R_{\mathrm{int}} I$, where OCV is a 7th-order polynomial fit and $R_{\mathrm{int}}$ is identified from the steady-state voltage--current characteristic.  Parameters: 9 (polynomial coefficients + $R_{\mathrm{int}}$).
    \item \textbf{Th\'{e}venin-1RC:}
    First-order ECM: $V = \mathrm{OCV} - R_0 I - V_{RC}$, $\dot{V}_{RC} = I/C_1 - V_{RC}/(R_1 C_1)$.  Parameters: 10 (polynomial OCV + $R_0, R_1, C_1$).
    \item \textbf{Th\'{e}venin-2RC:}
    Second-order ECM adding a diffusion RC branch: $V = \mathrm{OCV} - R_0 I - V_{RC,1} - V_{RC,2}$.  Parameters: 12.
\end{enumerate}
All ECM parameters are identified using differential-evolution global optimisation with identical settings (population 50, 1000 generations) and cost-function stabilisation (RC voltage clamping $|V_{RC}| \le \SI{2}{\volt}$ and finite-value guards) to ensure a fair comparison.

\paragraph{Note on data-driven baselines.}
We do not include purely data-driven models (e.g., LSTM, XGBoost) in the quantitative voltage-tracking comparison because (i)~such models require representative training data from each target operating condition, whereas the proposed framework generalises from a single constant-current discharge curve; (ii)~data-driven models do not provide the component-level power attribution or interpretable temperature--aging coupling that underpins the energy management strategy (\secref{sec:energy_mgmt}); and (iii)~the XJTU dataset's constant-current protocol does not supply the diverse load profiles needed to train a competitive data-driven predictor.
An LSTM inference time is included in \tabref{tab:efficiency} for computational-cost context only.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}\label{sec:metrics}
Three categories of metrics are employed.
First, \emph{voltage accuracy} is assessed via root-mean-square error (RMSE), mean absolute error (MAE), maximum absolute error (MaxErr), and mean relative error (MRE), computed point-wise over full discharge trajectories.
Second, \emph{TTE accuracy} is measured by the absolute and percentage deviation of ablated-model TTE from the full-model prediction.
Third, \emph{statistical robustness} is quantified through 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals obtained by Monte Carlo resampling: 500 parametric perturbation samples with $\pm5\%$ uniform noise on each parameter.

\paragraph{Why the three ECMs perform identically.}
The three ECM baselines yield nearly identical RMSE values (\SIrange{39.75}{39.81}{\milli\volt}).
This can be understood from the nature of the XJTU discharge data: constant-current (CC) protocols produce a monotonically declining voltage trajectory with no transient load steps.
Under CC conditions, the RC branch voltages in Th\'{e}venin models equilibrate within the first few seconds, after which the RC dynamics contribute a near-constant offset indistinguishable from adjusting $R_0$.
Consequently, the additional parameters in 1RC and 2RC models are structurally redundant for CC data and cannot improve upon the Rint baseline.

\paragraph{Source of the Shepherd advantage.}
The Shepherd model's $K/\mathrm{SOC}$ polarisation term provides a \emph{nonlinear} voltage correction that increases as the cell depletes.
This captures the concentration-gradient-driven overpotential that intensifies at low SOC---a physical effect that constant-valued ECM resistances cannot represent.
The exponential transient zone ($A$, $B$) further accounts for the initial voltage sag upon load application, which the Nernst model's logarithmic singularity erroneously amplifies at SOC extremes (explaining the NBM's high MaxErr of \SI{719.5}{\milli\volt}).

\paragraph{On the choice of a classical formulation.}
The Shepherd equation dates to 1965, yet its selection here is deliberate rather than retrograde.
More recent approaches---fractional-order ECMs, physics-informed neural networks, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS)-parameterised models---offer richer dynamics but at the cost of $>$20 parameters, specialised measurement equipment (EIS analysers), or GPU-accelerated training data that conflict with the portable-deployment objective of this work.
The present contribution is not a new voltage model \emph{per se}, but a \emph{system-level integration} that couples a right-sized voltage core with temperature, aging, and component-level load submodels to solve the discharge-side energy management problem.
We argue that model complexity should be \emph{commensurate with the application}: for the 1-second TTE update loop envisioned on an ARM Cortex-M microcontroller, the Shepherd formulation occupies the Pareto-optimal point of accuracy vs.\ computational cost (\tabref{tab:efficiency}), achieving lower RMSE than all ECM baselines with the fewest parameters (5 vs.\ 9--12).

\paragraph{Temperature coupling.}

\paragraph{Aging dynamics.}
Aging importance grows monotonically with cycle count---from exactly 0\% at $N=0$ (by construction) to $\sim$55\% at 800 cycles---and is nearly independent of temperature ($\pm 1\%$ variation across the three $T$ levels).

\paragraph{Polarisation.}
The $K$ term contributes only $0.8$--$0.9\%$ to TTE error, consistent with its primary role in mid-trajectory \emph{voltage shape} rather than integrated \emph{energy capacity}.

\paragraph{Non-additive coupling justifies the unified framework.}
Summing the individual ablation errors for, e.g., \SI{0}{\degreeCelsius} and 800 cycles ($70.6 + 53.4 + 21.9 + 0.8 = 146.7\%$) far exceeds the actual combined error observed in any single ablation, confirming that the submodel contributions are not independent.
The multiplicative coupling in \eqnsref{eq:Q0coupled}{eq:Rcoupled} produces interaction terms---e.g., temperature-reduced capacity amplifies aging-induced resistance---that a simple additive combination of standalone submodels would miss.
This non-additivity is precisely why a \emph{unified} framework is needed rather than a pipeline of independent corrections.

\subsection{Engineering implications and energy management}\label{sec:eng_mgmt}
\subsubsection{Engineering implications}\label{sec:engineering}
The results yield several actionable insights for power management:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Load-aware scheduling.}
    Since gaming depletes the battery $5.9\times$ faster than standby, OS-level awareness of load composition enables proactive low-battery warnings and adaptive quality-of-service adjustments.
    The component decomposition framework provides the necessary attribution for identifying which subsystem to throttle.
    \item \textbf{Temperature-aware operation.}
    The $\sim 70\%$ TTE overestimation at \SI{0}{\degreeCelsius} when temperature is ignored motivates incorporating ambient temperature into the OS battery estimator---a feature absent from most current mobile BMS implementations.
    \item \textbf{Health-aware budgeting.}
    After 800 cycles, TTE drops by $\sim 55\%$.
    Proactive notification and automatic power-profile switching for aged batteries can prevent unexpected shutdowns.
    \item \textbf{SOC management.}
    The calendar aging model (\eqnrefp{eq:calaging}) quantitatively shows that maintaining SOC within the 20\%--80\% range extends battery lifespan by reducing parasitic SEI growth at high electrode potentials.
\end{enumerate}



\subsubsection{Adaptive energy management optimisation}\label{sec:energy_mgmt}
To demonstrate the framework's utility as a \emph{model-predictive control} (MPC) engine for energy conversion and management, we formulate the discharge-side power scheduling problem as a receding-horizon optimisation and compare it against a rule-based threshold baseline.

\paragraph{MPC formulation.}
Let $T_{\mathrm{des}}$ denote the user's desired runtime (e.g., $T_{\mathrm{des}} = \SI{6}{\hour}$).
At each control instant $t_k$ (period $\Delta t_c = \SI{120}{\second}$), the MPC solves:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpc}
\min_{\mathbf{u}_k} \; J_k = \sum_{j=0}^{N_p - 1} \Bigl[
  \underbrace{\alpha \Bigl(\frac{P_{\max} - u_{k+j}}{P_{\max}}\Bigr)^{\!2}}_{\text{QoS loss}}
+ \underbrace{\beta \bigl(\hat{s}_{k+j} - s_{k+j}^{\mathrm{ref}}\bigr)^{2}}_{\text{SOC tracking}}
+ \underbrace{\gamma \Bigl(\frac{u_{k+j} - u_{k+j-1}}{P_{\max}}\Bigr)^{\!2}}_{\text{smoothness}}
\Bigr]
\end{equation}
subject to:
\begin{align}
\hat{s}_{k+j+1} &= \hat{s}_{k+j} - \frac{u_{k+j}}{V_{\mathrm{Shep}}(\hat{s}_{k+j}, T, N) \cdot Q_0(T,N)} \,\Delta t_c, \label{eq:mpc_dyn} \\
P_{\min} &\le u_{k+j} \le P_{\max}, \quad j = 0,\ldots,N_p{-}1, \label{eq:mpc_bounds}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{u}_k = [u_k,\ldots,u_{k+N_p-1}]^\top$ is the power decision vector over a prediction horizon of $N_p = 10$ steps ($\SI{1200}{\second}$), $\hat{s}$ denotes the predicted SOC, $V_{\mathrm{Shep}}$ is the Shepherd voltage model (\eqnrefp{eq:shepherd}), and $s^{\mathrm{ref}}(t) = s(t_k) - [s(t_k) - s_{\mathrm{cut}}] \cdot (t - t_k)/(T_{\mathrm{des}} - t_k)$ is a linear reference trajectory from the current SOC to the cutoff threshold $s_{\mathrm{cut}} = 0.05$ at $t = T_{\mathrm{des}}$.
Only the first action $u_k^*$ is applied; the horizon then shifts forward and the optimisation is re-solved---the standard receding-horizon principle.
The weights $(\alpha, \beta, \gamma) = (0.3, 500, 0.2)$ are selected to prioritise SOC tracking (ensuring the battery reaches $T_{\mathrm{des}}$) while maximising quality of service.
\eqnref{eq:mpc} is solved via L-BFGS-B with box constraints (\eqnrefp{eq:mpc_bounds}), requiring $< 60$ iterations per step.

\paragraph{Threshold baseline.}
For comparison, a two-stage rule-based strategy applies fixed power reductions at predetermined SOC levels: Stage~1 (SOC~$\le 20\%$) reduces power from \SI{4.303}{\watt} (gaming) to \SI{1.73}{\watt} (reading-level); Stage~2 (SOC~$\le 10\%$) further reduces to \SI{0.74}{\watt} (standby).

A user begins online gaming ($P_{\max} = \SI{4.303}{\watt}$) at full charge with $T_{\mathrm{des}} = \SI{6}{\hour}$.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The MPC formulation offers three structural advantages over threshold strategies.
First, the power trajectory is \emph{continuous} rather than piecewise-constant, enabling smoother quality-of-service degradation that is less perceptible to the user.
Second, the \emph{target runtime is an explicit input} ($T_{\mathrm{des}}$), allowing the OS to expose a ``last until'' setting that adapts the throttling profile automatically---unlike fixed-threshold approaches that are insensitive to the user's remaining schedule.
Third, because the prediction model (\eqnrefp{eq:mpc_dyn}) inherits the temperature and aging coupling of the Shepherd framework, the MPC adapts its power trajectory to the battery's current health: an aged cell ($N = 800$) or a cold environment ($T = \SI{0}{\degreeCelsius}$) triggers earlier and deeper throttling \emph{without any parameter re-tuning}, whereas rule-based thresholds remain static.

\paragraph{Connection to energy conversion and management.}
From an energy-system perspective, the MPC strategy represents \emph{demand-side energy management} at the device level---analogous to the economy-conscious charging optimisation studied by Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2019ECM,Liu2018ECM} in the EV domain.
Their coupled electrothermal-aging models jointly optimise charging time, temperature rise, and degradation cost; the present work extends this model-predictive philosophy to the \emph{discharge} side, using the Shepherd model as the internal prediction engine for load reduction.
The component-level power decomposition (\eqnrefp{eq:ptotal}) identifies \emph{which} subsystem to throttle---a capability that mirrors the component-level thermal attribution in battery thermal management systems~\cite{Mama2025}.
At the aggregate scale, the framework's ability to predict TTE under arbitrary $(T, N)$ conditions could support coordinated charge scheduling across large device fleets, contributing to peak-shaving and demand-response objectives in smart-grid scenarios.

\paragraph{Energy efficiency quantification.}
Over the baseline depletion window (\SI{3.8}{\hour}), the MPC consumes approximately \SI{14.2}{\watt\hour} vs.\ the baseline's \SI{16.3}{\watt\hour}, yielding a per-cycle energy reduction of $\sim$13\% at matched service duration.
If the full MPC session (\SI{5.99}{\hour}) is considered, the energy efficiency---defined as useful runtime per unit energy consumed---improves by approximately \SI{56}{\percent}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpc_eff}
\eta_{\mathrm{MPC}} = \frac{t_{\mathrm{MPC}} / E_{\mathrm{MPC}}}{t_{\mathrm{base}} / E_{\mathrm{base}}} = \frac{5.99 / 16.5}{3.79 / 16.3} \approx 1.56.
\end{equation}
Note that these figures are simulated values under idealised load assumptions; real-world gains will depend on load-profile accuracy and user acceptance of quality-of-service degradation.

\section{Conclusions and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper presented a unified continuous-time simulation framework for portable-device battery TTE prediction that integrates a modified Shepherd voltage model, five-component power decomposition, logistic capacity--temperature and Arrhenius resistance--temperature coupling, and power-law cycle/calendar aging into a single differential-algebraic system.
The key findings are as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The Shepherd-based formulation achieves a voltage RMSE of \SI{17.85 \pm 0.44}{\milli\volt}, reducing error by \SI{75.5}{\percent} relative to the Nernst baseline and \SI{55}{\percent} relative to Th\'{e}venin-1RC on XJTU Batch-1.
    \item Cross-batch validation (Batch-1$\to$Batch-2, no re-fitting) confirms generalisability across C-rates, with a statistically non-significant RMSE gap ($-1.22$\,mV, 95\% CI spanning zero).
    \item Dynamic-load validation on the NASA random-walk dataset~\cite{Bole2014} yields a voltage RMSE of \SI{63.5 \pm 27.6}{\milli\volt} across 200 cycles with currents spanning 0.5--\SI{4}{\ampere}, using parameters identified solely from constant-current data---demonstrating that the Shepherd formulation transfers to time-varying loads without re-identification.
    \item Multi-condition ablation across a $3 \times 3$ temperature--aging grid quantifies each module's contribution: temperature coupling up to \SI{70}{\percent}, aging up to \SI{55}{\percent}, component decomposition up to \SI{43}{\percent}, and polarisation $<\SI{1}{\percent}$ of TTE error.
    \item A receding-horizon model-predictive control (MPC) energy management strategy, formulated as a constrained optimisation with SOC-tracking and quality-of-service objectives (\eqnrefp{eq:mpc}), extends simulated runtime by up to \SI{58}{\percent} (TTE $= \SI{6.0}{\hour}$ vs.\ $\SI{3.8}{\hour}$ baseline) and improves energy efficiency by \SI{56}{\percent}, outperforming the threshold-based strategy ($+40\%$) by an additional \SI{18}{pp} while producing a smooth, continuous power profile.
    \item A computation cost of \SI{0.009}{\milli\second} per simulated second (\tabref{tab:efficiency}) confirms suitability for real-time deployment on embedded hardware, including ARM Cortex-M class low-power microcontrollers (\SI{<1}{\milli\watt} computational overhead).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
This work is positioned as a \emph{simulation framework} validated on cell-level electrochemical data.
The voltage model and its parameters are fitted and validated on real cell data---both under constant-current conditions (XJTU dataset) and under dynamic random-walk loads (NASA dataset~\cite{Bole2014})---while the component-level load profiles are synthetic, constructed from published hardware power measurements~\cite{Carroll2010,Chen2014,Lu2008}.
Although the TTE predictions under these synthetic loads cannot be directly verified against real-device measurements, the dual-dataset voltage validation (CC and dynamic) ensures a sound electrochemical foundation.
Importantly, the synthetic load profiles are not arbitrary---they are constructed by combining individually measured component powers from peer-reviewed hardware studies, and they are applied as inputs to an independently validated electrochemical model.
This decoupled validation approach (electrochemistry on real cell data, loads from literature) is common in the battery modelling literature~\cite{Fuller2014,Mama2025} and provides a rigorous basis for scenario exploration.
The framework's modular load interface (\ssref{sec:power}) is designed as a plug-and-play architecture: replacing the synthetic profiles with real telemetry data (e.g., from Android's BatteryStats API or iOS's Energy Diagnostics) requires only substituting the $\xi_i(t)$ time series, with no changes to the electrochemical or thermal submodels.
Additional limitations include:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Quasi-static temperature:}  The model assumes a constant ambient temperature per simulation; rapid thermal transients from SoC self-heating are not captured.
    As emphasised in the multi-scale modelling review by Mama et al.~\cite{Mama2025}, electrochemical-thermal coupled (ECTC) models---which solve a heat-generation equation alongside the electrochemical dynamics---are essential for accurately predicting temperature-dependent degradation under sustained high-power loads.
    The absence of such coupling in the present framework means that self-heating-induced capacity reduction during intensive usage phases (e.g., gaming at \SI{4.3}{\watt}) may be underestimated, potentially leading to optimistic TTE predictions at high power levels.
    Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2019ECM} demonstrated that even a lumped two-state thermal model (internal and surface temperature) significantly improves the fidelity of coupled electrothermal predictions; Jeon and Baek~\cite{Jeon2011ECM} and Liu et al.~\cite{Liu2017ECM} further established thermal modelling methodologies for lithium-ion cells during discharge. Incorporating a similar thermal ODE into the present framework is a natural next step.
    \item \emph{Quasi-static voltage model:}  The dynamic validation (\secref{sec:dynamic_val}) demonstrates reasonable accuracy under random-walk loads (RMSE $\sim$\SI{64}{\milli\volt}), but the algebraic Shepherd formulation does not capture RC-type transient relaxation dynamics.  Incorporating a Th\'{e}venin RC branch or re-identifying parameters on standardised dynamic profiles (e.g., DST, UDDS) could further reduce the dynamic RMSE.
    \item \emph{No Peukert-type rate derating:}  The present model treats nominal capacity $Q_0$ as a function of temperature and aging only (\eqnrefp{eq:Q0T}); it does not incorporate a Peukert-style correction for the reduction in effective capacity at high discharge rates~\cite{Fuller2014}.
    For portable-device cells operating predominantly between 0.3C and 1.5C, this omission has limited practical impact; however, under pulsed high-C-rate loads (e.g., camera flash, peak SoC bursts), the model may slightly overestimate the available capacity and hence TTE.
    \item \emph{Homogeneous aging:}  Cell-to-cell variability and electrode-level spatial heterogeneity are not modelled.
    \item \emph{Single chemistry:}  Results are on NMC/graphite cells; other chemistries (LFP, NCA, solid-state) require re-parameterisation.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Future work.}
Planned extensions include:
(i)~end-to-end validation using real device power traces with concurrent battery telemetry, building on the dynamic-load validation presented in \secref{sec:dynamic_val};
(ii)~coupled electro-thermal modelling via a lumped thermal ODE for self-heating;
(iii)~stochastic load modelling using Markov-chain usage profiles;
(iv)~integration into Android's PowerManagerService or Linux power governance for on-device field testing;
(v)~hybrid physics-data correction using a lightweight neural residual network to compensate for unmodelled transient dynamics (e.g., the RC relaxation effects observed in the NASA dynamic validation);
and (vi)~exploration of aggregated distributed portable storage for vehicle-to-grid (V2G) style demand response, where billions of portable devices collectively contribute to grid frequency regulation and peak shaving through coordinated charge scheduling.

\section{Proof of \propref{prop:mono}}\label{app:proof}
\begin{proof}
For fixed SOC, define $F = E_0 + A\exp\!\bigl(-BQ_0(1-\mathrm{SOC})\bigr) > 0$ and $\alpha = K/\mathrm{SOC} + R > 0$.
The Shepherd voltage (\eqnrefp{eq:shepherd}) is affine in~$I$: $V_{\mathrm{term}}(I) = F - \alpha I$.
Hence $g(I) = I\,V_{\mathrm{term}}(I) = FI - \alpha I^2$ is a concave quadratic with $g(0) = 0$ and a unique maximum $g^* = F^2/(4\alpha)$ at $I^* = F/(2\alpha)$.
For any $P_{\mathrm{total}} \in (0, g^*]$, the equation $g(I) = P_{\mathrm{total}}$ has discriminant $\Delta = F^2 - 4\alpha P_{\mathrm{total}} \ge 0$, yielding two roots $I_{\pm} = (F \pm \sqrt{\Delta})/(2\alpha)$.
The smaller root $I_- \le I^*$ gives $V_{\mathrm{term}}(I_-) = F - \alpha I_- \ge F/2 > 0$, a physically valid operating point.
Since $g'(I) = F - 2\alpha I > 0$ for $I < I^*$, $g$ is strictly increasing on $[0, I^*]$ and $I_-$ is the unique root in this interval.
\end{proof}
